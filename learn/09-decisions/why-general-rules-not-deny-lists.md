# Why General Rules, Not Deny Lists?

[HEAD Agent Core](../../README.md) / [Learn](../README.md) / [Decisions](README.md) / Why General Rules, Not Deny Lists?

## Problem

Each operational failure invites a new prohibition. Over time, an instruction set can become a catalog of past accidents that is difficult to apply to a new situation.

## Attempted Alternative

Respond to every failure with a narrower ban, exception, or mandatory wording rule. This seems safer because it directly names the behavior that went wrong.

## Observed Failure

**Historical record.** Shared Core history records a deliberate refinement from a longer, rule-heavy form toward compact ownership and reasoning principles, alongside planning skills that hold detailed conditional procedure.

**Operational observation.** A deny list can prevent a known bad move without helping an agent recognize the broader invariant. It grows with incidents, conflicts internally, and leaves unfamiliar cases uncovered.

**Generalized failure.** A new task resembles none of the listed exceptions. The agent follows every literal ban but still copies irrelevant context into a brief because no rule explains that context should be selected by the decisions it changes.

## Current Decision

Put portable reasoning and ownership principles in the always-applicable Core: compose context deliberately, make the work model explicit, preserve coherent ownership, continue from canon, respect decision ownership, and build confidence into work. Put detailed, conditional procedures in on-demand skills. Improve the general model when a recurring failure reveals a missing invariant.

## Related Theory

**Related theory.** Principle-based governance and rule-versus-standard design explain the trade-off. A standard supports judgment in novel cases; a rule supplies predictable constraint. The current model uses both, assigning each to the layer where it is useful.

## Current Limitation

General principles can be interpreted too broadly and do not replace hard safety boundaries, legal requirements, or exact protocols. A principle also cannot repair missing facts. Where precise behavior is necessary, the relevant procedure or approval boundary remains explicit.

## Takeaway

Turn recurring failures into reusable reasoning, not an ever-growing list of remembered prohibitions. Keep exact procedures where exactness is genuinely required.
